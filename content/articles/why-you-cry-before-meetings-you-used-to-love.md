# Why You Cry Before Meetings You Used to Love

**The tears aren't the problem. They're a measurable signal from a nervous system that has been running on emergency power for too long.**

You close your laptop lid before the Zoom window renders. Camera off, mic muted. The meeting you used to chair — the cross-functional sync you actually designed — starts in ninety seconds, and your chest is tight in a way that feels both humiliating and unfamiliar. Last quarter, you walked in sharp. This morning, you're blinking hard in your home office, trying to calculate whether concealer will hold if you let one tear through. Something in the circuitry has shifted, and it shifted without your permission.

If you're a senior professional who has started crying — in bathrooms, parking lots, the four minutes before a standing meeting — you are not having a personal failure. You are exhibiting one of the most clinically documented symptoms of chronic workplace stress: a breakdown in the brain's capacity to down-regulate emotional responses on command.

## What's actually happening in your brain

The tears are not random. They are the visible surface of a neural event that neuroscientists have mapped with some precision.

In a now-foundational PLOS One study, Amita Golkar and colleagues at the Karolinska Institute investigated how chronic work-related stress alters brain function and emotional processing. What they found should be required reading for anyone in a demanding senior role. When participants were told to maintain or up-regulate their emotional responses, the burnout and control groups showed similar startle responses. But during the down-regulate condition, the burnout group not only exhibited a greater stress response than controls, but also reported less success at implementing the emotional regulation instructions.

Translated: people with burnout could feel emotions normally. What they could not do was turn the volume down when the situation required it.

The structural reasons are observable on scans. The amygdala — critical for evoking fear and anxiety — is enlarged in people with occupational stress. Functional connectivity between the amygdala and the prefrontal cortex was weaker in burnout patients, while connections to the insula were stronger. The prefrontal cortex is the executive brake. When its wiring to the threat-detection system degrades, small provocations bypass the filter.

> The tears are not a character flaw. They are the visible surface of weakened connectivity between the part of your brain that feels and the part of your brain that governs.

This is compounded by what cortisol does over time. Research from Stanford's Laboratory of Stress and Resilience has shown that even moderate chronic stress reduces grey matter density in the prefrontal cortex over time. Leaders begin to experience decision hesitation, reduced creativity, irritability in high-stakes meetings, and cognitive fatigue by mid-afternoon. The same meetings you used to conduct at full capacity now cost you measurably more, because the organ running them has less tissue to work with.

## Why it's the meetings you used to love

The specificity matters. You are not crying before meetings you dreaded — you're crying before the ones that used to energize you. This is a diagnostic clue, not a coincidence.

Meetings you cared about are the ones where you had identity investment: the strategy review you built, the team you hired, the client relationship you cultivated. The WHO's classification of burnout has three dimensions: feelings of energy depletion or exhaustion, increased mental distance from one's job, feelings of negativism related to one's job, and reduced professional efficiency resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed. That second dimension — cynicism or "mental distance" — is most painful precisely where prior engagement was highest. The gap between who you were in that room and who you are now is the gap your body is registering.

There is also a pattern-recognition explanation. When your energy reserves are depleted, the part of your brain responsible for emotional regulation simply has less fuel to work with. Frustrations that you could normally absorb become the thing that makes you cry at your desk. The meetings you loved were also high-stakes — which means high cognitive and emotional load. Low-stakes meetings don't tax a depleted system. The ones you cared about do.

## The data says you're not the anomaly

If crying before a 9 a.m. call feels like personal collapse, the statistics suggest otherwise. In Grant Thornton's 2024 State of Work in America survey, 51% of respondents suffered burnout in the past year — a 15 percentage-point increase from the prior year. The top causes: mental and emotional stress at 63%, followed by long hours at 54%.

The gender and seniority patterns are sharper still. Women report burnout at 59% versus men at 46% — a significant gender gap. And mid-level employees report the highest burnout, at 54%, caught between senior direction and frontline workers. If you are a woman in mid-senior leadership, you are sitting in the statistical epicenter.

Sector matters too. In the United States, 77% of respondents say they have experienced burnout at their current job, with more than half citing more than one occurrence. The Society for Human Resource Management found that 45% feel "emotionally drained" from their work, and 51% feel "used up" at the end of the workday. Emotional drainage is not a metaphor. It is the phenomenology of a dysregulated HPA axis.

## The organizational gaslighting problem

Here is the second trap: once the crying starts, the response from the system usually makes it worse.

Business school researchers have begun documenting this. In a widely discussed IMD analysis, a senior researcher — a potential successor to the Head of R&D — burst into tears during a heated restructuring discussion. Of the six other people present, all men, not one acknowledged her emotions. The following day, her boss advised her to avoid crying during meetings, warning that it could make her look weak and even jeopardize her promotion prospects.

The advice is common. It is also counterproductive. When someone cries, it strips away the veneer of politeness and reveals that there are issues that should be addressed. Telling women or men to hold back their tears often leads to bottled-up stress and anxiety, which can manifest as burnout, disengagement, or even resignation. Confronting these issues is far healthier for an organization than sweeping them under the rug.

The cultural script — "compose yourself" — asks you to apply exactly the down-regulation capacity that burnout has physically impaired. It's the equivalent of telling someone with a sprained ankle to walk it off.

## What This Means For You

A few things follow from the evidence, none of them involving resilience apps.
