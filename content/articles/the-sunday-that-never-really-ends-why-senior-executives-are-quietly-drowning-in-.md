# The Sunday That Never Really Ends: Why Senior Executives Are Quietly Drowning in Anticipatory Dread

**Chronic Sunday night anxiety isn't a character flaw or a scheduling failure. For senior leaders, it's a reliable diagnostic signal — and one the data suggests most of you are ignoring.**

The dread arrives, on average, at 3:54 p.m. A survey of 2,000 adults found that this feeling is defined as anxiety (32%) or dread (17%) over what to expect for the following week, and shared that it sets in around 3:54 p.m. For most people, it's a mood — an unpleasant but tolerable end-of-weekend tax. For senior executives, it is frequently something more corrosive: a weekly, body-based forecast that the working life they built no longer fits the person doing it. Most leaders treat the feeling as noise to be managed. The research suggests it is signal.

## The Executive Paradox

The cultural narrative about Sunday night anxiety frames it as a young person's problem. Survey data partly confirms that — 74 percent of Gen Z respondents experience Sunday scaries at least once a month, a higher rate than any other age group — but that framing obscures something more interesting happening at the top of organizations.

UK HR software firm CIPHR's research on leaders found that dread concentrates, not dissipates, with seniority. Almost 10% of leaders aged between 45 and 54 have suffered from the Sunday scaries every week in the past year, compared to 4% of 25-to-34-year-olds, 7% of 35-to-44-year-olds, and 1% of those over 55. That's a near-tenfold gap between mid-career Gen X executives and their counterparts just a decade older — a pattern that cannot be explained by generational temperament alone.

The effect compounds with scope. Senior executives and leaders at bigger enterprises with over 1,000 employees were more than twice as likely to report experiencing the Sunday scaries multiple times a month, compared to those at SMEs with 101 to 250 employees. Women in leadership carry more of it than men. While 4% of male senior managers experience the Sunday scaries every week, this jumps to 7% for women. And remote work, often sold as the antidote, is not one: the study found remote workers are worse-hit.

> The Sunday scaries are not a junior-professional affliction that executives have outgrown. They are an occupational hazard that scales with responsibility.

## Why Seniority Amplifies the Signal

Anticipatory anxiety is, mechanically, a brain doing what brains do. When anticipatory anxiety kicks in, your body releases cortisol and adrenaline, the same hormones that activate during a "fight or flight" response. Research also suggests that the transition from weekend to workweek disrupts your sense of autonomy. For senior leaders, both variables sit at the extremes.

Autonomy, at first glance, should be a protective factor — executives control more of their calendars than most. But the kind of autonomy that matters neurologically is the ability to predict and influence the week ahead, and at the executive level, the job is largely to absorb unpredictability on behalf of others. The result is a mismatch: formal authority without felt control. The body keeps its own ledger.

Occupational psychology research suggests something similar. Human energy follows a weekly rhythm — dropping on Monday and gradually recovering toward Friday — and people who can anticipate something positive about their work week experience a smaller Monday dip. When the role itself offers fewer genuinely anticipated moments — when meetings feel procedural, outcomes feel preordained, and the calendar reads as obligation rather than contribution — that Monday dip deepens into something structural.

This is where the executive paradox becomes clearest. Junior employees dread Monday because they lack power. Senior employees dread Monday because they have run out of meaningful ways to use it.

## The Physiology of a Losing Transaction

The casual framing — "Sunday scaries" — makes the phenomenon sound like a mood. The physiology tells a different story.

According to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine, nearly 80 per cent of US adults say they have trouble falling asleep on Sundays compared to other nights. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found that anxiety related to the upcoming week can significantly disrupt sleep patterns, estimating that up to 76% of people experience some form of sleep disruption on Sunday nights. Sleep debt accrued on Sunday doesn't clear by Wednesday; it compounds, degrading exactly the cognitive capacities — judgment, emotional regulation, strategic pattern-recognition — that senior roles demand.

The hormonal cost is measurable. A 2025 study of older adults found that Monday-specific anxiety led to 23 per cent higher cortisol levels — the body's main stress hormone — an elevation that does not merely describe a bad mood but corresponds to real cardiovascular, immune, and metabolic risk over time. For executives already running multi-year cortisol deficits, a recurring Sunday spike is not an inconvenience. It is a slow-motion health event.

And the behaviors people use to blunt the dread often deepen it. Twenty-seven percent of men say they use alcohol or substances to relax on Sundays, compared with 13% of women — a noticeable gap that also highlights how many people are still relying on strategies that might make Monday worse, not better. Alcohol the night before a high-stakes Monday trades emotional processing for a sedative effect that wears off at 3 a.m.

## When Dread Becomes Diagnosis

The most useful reframe available to senior professionals is to stop treating the Sunday feeling as a symptom to suppress and start treating it as information to read.

Sometimes the signal is workload, which can be redesigned. In 2024, Talker Research conducted a survey that revealed that the average American experiences the "Sunday Scaries" at least 36 times per year and 55% reported general burnout.
