# The Quiet Dissolution: When a Long Career Stops Fitting

**After two decades in one field, a career doesn't sit on top of you. It fuses with you. When it stops fitting, the experience isn't burnout — it's identity collapse, and the two require different responses.**

Burnout is a workload problem. Identity collapse is a structural one.

Burnout responds to rest, boundaries, and reduced hours. Identity collapse doesn't. If your professional role has been your main source of meaning for twenty years and it starts to feel hollow, more vacation won't fix it. What's disappearing isn't energy — it's the self that was built around the job.

Clinical psychologist Janna Koretz calls the underlying condition career enmeshment: fusion of self and role to the point that the boundary between them dissolves. The concept adapts Salvador Minuchin's 1970s family-systems work — swap "family members" for "self and job title."

The signs are specific, and mostly quiet:

- Mood tracks work performance one-to-one. A bad meeting becomes an existential event.
- Every conversation routes back to work. Other dimensions of self have thinned.
- Time off triggers anxiety rather than relief.
- You can articulate your opinions on your field, your politics, your family role — but the question "what do you enjoy outside of those?" lands in silence.

## Why twenty years is the threshold.

James Marcia's identity framework (1966) names the precursor: identity foreclosure — commitment to a profession without prior self-exploration. Most people who entered law, medicine, consulting, or finance in their early twenties chose the path before the person was formed. Foreclosed identities correlate with lower openness to experience and higher resistance to change — which is precisely why long-tenured professionals struggle most with AI disruption and flattening hierarchies. The fusion that produced excellence now produces brittleness.

## Scale.

Mercer's 2024 Global Talent Trends report (~12,000 respondents) found over 80% of employees at risk of burnout, with the burden rising by seniority. 56% of leaders report being burned out, up from 52% the year before. How much of that is genuine burnout and how much is misdiagnosed identity collapse isn't clear — but the interventions being prescribed (rest, boundaries) won't work on the second group.

## The distinction that matters.

Burnout asks: how do I recover? Identity collapse asks: who am I without this? The first is logistical. The second is the actual work.
